Jaffa City



Jaffa (Hebrew: יָפוֹ Yafu) is one of the oldest and most important historical cities in Palestine.
Today it is located within the Israeli municipality of "Tel Aviv - Jaffa", on the eastern coast of
the Mediterranean Sea - according to the Israeli administrative division. It is 55 km from Jerusalem
to the west. For a long time, it occupied an important position among the major Palestinian cities in
terms of area, population and strategic location, until the date of the Nakba in 1948, and the displacement
of most of its Arab residents. Today, it is inhabited by approximately 60,000 people, most of whom are Jews,
and an Arab minority of Muslims and Christians.


The city was founded by the Canaanites in the fourth millennium BC, and since that date it has been an important
commercial center for the region. The Assyrian, Babylonian and Persian rule BC, and its link with the Greek
civilization was close, then it entered the rule of the Romans and the Byzantines, and its inhabitants were among
the first to embrace Christianity. One of the most important events that the city witnessed was when the Prophet
Yunus descended on its shores in the eighth century B.C. to board a ship from it, destined for Tarshish. When the
Islamic conquest entered Palestine, Amr Ibn Al-Aas conquered Jaffa in the same year that Omar Ibn Al-Khattab entered
Jerusalem. Jaffa continued to occupy this important position among the cities of Palestine, and remained a major
commercial center, a port for Jerusalem and a port for pilgrims. During the Ottoman period, specifically in 1885,
the first municipal council was established in Jaffa.
