Safed City



Safed (Hebrew: צפת) is one of the historical cities of Palestine. The city is located in the Galilee region.
Today, it is in the northern region of Israel. It is also considered one of the country's highest cities, about
900 meters above sea level. It is located 134 km north of Jerusalem, overlooking the Sea of ​​Galilee and the Beisan
meadow to the southeast, and Mount Al-Jarmaq to the west. The area of ​​the city is 29,248 km², and it is inhabited
today by about 28,000 people - most of them are Jews, after the displacement of its Arab people in the 1948 war.
It was mentioned in Jewish religious literature and among Roman historians, and since the sixteenth century, with
the fall of Andalusia and the expulsion of the Jews from the Iberian Peninsula, arose It has a community of Sephardic
Jews and followers of the Kabbalah in particular, which made it one of the four holy cities of Judaism at that time.
In the Ottoman era, it was the center of the Sanjak of Safad belonging to the Eyalet of Damascus, and during the period
of the British Mandate over Palestine, it was the center of the district of Safed.

During the archaeological excavations around the Citadel of Safed (The Orphan Tower), urban remains from the Iron Age and
Bronze Age burials were found. The city is not mentioned in the ancient sources, so it seems that it was a small town in
pre-Christian times. The Book of Judges mentions a Canaanite city called “Safat” (Judges 1:17), but it appears from the
description of its place that it fell in the Negev and has no connection with the contemporary city of Safed. The first
undoubted reference to the city of Safed is in the books of the historian Josephus Flavius, who mentions it as one of the
sites that fortified in preparation for the failed Jewish rebellion against the Roman Empire in the first century AD.
